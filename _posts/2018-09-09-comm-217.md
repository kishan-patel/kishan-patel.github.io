### Chapter 1 - Financial Statements and Business Decisions
<b>Shareholder</b>:  
Investors who buy small percentages of large corporations. They hope to make money in two ways:  
1- Expect to receive a portion of what the company earns in the form of cash payments called dividends  
2- Hope to eventually share their share of the company at a higher price than they paid  

<b>Creditors</b>:  
Lend money to a company for a specific length of time. Gain by charging interest on the money the lend.  

<b>The Business Operations</b>:  
1- Purchase products and access to services demanded by the customer  
2- Market the product and services available for sale  
3- Sell products and services to customers  
4- Collect cash from customer and pay suppliers  

<b>Internal Decision Makers</b>:  
Mangers are internal decision makers who need information about the company's business activities to control and improve the operating, investing, and financing activities of the firm.

<b>External Decision Makers</b>:  
Shareholder and creditors are external decision makers who need information about the business activities to assess whether the company will be able to pay back its debts with interest and pay dividends.

<b>Accounting System</b>:  
Collects and processess useful financial information about an organization and reports that information to decision makers.  

<b>Examples of Business Activities</b>:  
1- Financing Activities: borrowing or paying back money to lenders and receiving additional funds from shareholders, or paying them dividends  
2- Investing Activities: buying or selling items, such as retail stores, buildings, and equipment used to store and then distribute goods and services to customers  
3- Operating Activities: the day-to-day process of obtaining products and services from suppliers, storing and then delivering them to customers, collecting cash from customers, and paying suppliers  

<b>Four Basic Financial Statements</b>:  
1- Statement of Financial Position: Purpose is to report the financial position (amount of assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity) of an accounting entity at a particular point in time  
2- Statement of Earnings (or income statement): Reports the net earnings achieved during the accounting period  
3- Statement of Changes in Equity: Reports the way that elements of comprehensive income, dividends, and changes to contributed capital affected the financial position of the company during the accounting period   
4- Statement of Cash Flows: Reports inflows (receipts) and outflows (payments) of cash during the accounting period in the operating, investing, and financing categories

<u>Cash flows from operating activities</u>: Include cash flows that are directly related to generate earnings. For example, when customers pay Canadian Tire for the product and services it delivered to them.
<u>Cash flows from investing activities</u>: Includes cash flows related to the acquisition or sale of the company's property, equipment, and investments.  
<u>Cash flows from financing activities</u>: Is directly related to the financing of the company. It involves receipt and payments of cash to investors and creditors.  

<b>Relationships among the Four Financial Statements</b>:  
1- Net earnings from the statement of earnings results in an increase in ending retained earnings on the statement of changes in equity    
2- Ending retained earnings from the statement of changes in equity is one of the three components of shareholders' equity on the statement of financial position.  
3- The change in cash on the statement of cash flows added to the case balance at the beginning of the year equals the balance of cash at the end of the year, which appears on the statement of financial position

<b>International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)</b>:  
Encompasses broad principles, specific rules, practices, and conventions of general application that are used by organizations to record transactions and report financial statement information to interested users.

<b>Notes to the Financial Statements</b>:  
All financial statements should be accompanied by notes. There are three types of notes:  
1- Accounting rules used to prepare F/S  
2- Additional details for items in the F/S  
3- Additional information items NOT in the financial statement (ex: risks)  

<b>Reporting Standards</b>:  
GAAP - Generally Accepted Accounting Principles  
IFRS - International Financial Reporting Standards  

<b>How releasing Financial Statements affects companies</b>:  
1- Price of shares  
2- Bonuses for managers and employees  
3- Release of competitive information to competitors  

<b>Steps taken by management to ensure accuracy of a company's financial info</b>:  
1- Maintain a system of control  
2- Hire outside independent auditors  
3- Form a committee of the board of directors to review above two  

<b>Audit</b>:  
1- Ensures financial reports comply with GAAP  
2- Examine the transactions incorporated in the financial statements  
3- Express an opinion as to the fairness of presentation of financial information  

<b>Business Entities</b>:  
1- Sole Proprietorship: owned by a single individual  
2- Partnership: owned by two or more individuals  
3- Corporation: ownership represented by shares of stock   

<b>Advantages/Disadvantages of a Corporation</b>:  
Advantages: Limited liability, Continuity of life, Ease of transfer of ownership, Opportunity to raise large amounts of money  
Disadvantages: Double taxation

</font>
<font color="blue">
<pre>
<b>Equation</b>: Beginning retained earnings + Net earnings - Dividends paid = Ending retained earnings

<b>Beginning retained earnings</b>: Accumulated net earnings of the company through the years which have been re-invested in the company

<b>Net earnings</b>: Profit (or loss) for the current year

<b>Dividends</b>: Amount paid to shareholders
</pre>
</font>
